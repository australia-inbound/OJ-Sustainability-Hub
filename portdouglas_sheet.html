Destination overview

Location & character
Far North Queensland, roughly an hour's drive north of Cairns. The only place on Earth where two World Heritage areas meet — the Wet Tropics rainforest (inscribed 1988) and the Great Barrier Reef. The Daintree is estimated at over 180 million years old, among the oldest continuously surviving tropical rainforests anywhere.
Access
Cairns Airport (CNS) is the gateway, about an hour's drive south of Port Douglas via the Captain Cook Highway. North of the Daintree River, a cable ferry — not a bridge — is the only crossing, a deliberate decision that limits development pressure on the rainforest side.
Best season
May–Oct (dry season) — most comfortable, best visibility. Nov–Apr (wet season) — lush, dramatic, more humid, and marine stinger season on beaches (stinger suits required for swimming).

02

Eastern Kuku Yalanji & Yirraganydji Country

Traditional Owners
The Eastern Kuku Yalanji are custodians of the Daintree and Mossman Gorge to the north; the Yirraganydji of Port Douglas and the coast to the south. Both have cared for this rainforest-reef landscape for over 50,000 years.
The 2021 Daintree handback
On 29 September 2021, 160,213 hectares — including the Daintree, Ngalba Bulal, Black Mountain and Hope Islands National Parks — were formally transferred to the Jabalbina Yalanji Aboriginal Corporation on behalf of the Eastern Kuku Yalanji people. The land is now jointly managed under an Indigenous Management Agreement between Jabalbina, the Wet Tropics Management Authority and QPWS.
Cultural experiences
The Mossman Gorge Centre has maintained an 80–90% Indigenous employment rate since opening in 2012, and the Kuku Yalanji Dreamtime Walks — running continuously since 1987 — are the single best way for clients to understand the rainforest through Kuku Yalanji eyes.
Priority recommendation
Include the Mossman Gorge Dreamtime Walk in every Port Douglas & Daintree itinerary. It pairs a genuine cultural experience with the region's flagship rainforest site, and supports a centre that is itself a strong local-employment story.
03

Sustainability & current status

Australia's first ECO Certified Destination
The Douglas Shire became the world's first destination awarded Ecotourism Australia's ECO Destination Certification, on 4 December 2019 — meeting more than 80 of 100 criteria under the internationally recognised Green Destinations Standard. This is a genuine point of difference, not marketing language, and worth leading with in client conversations.
A deep local commitment
The region's sustainability story goes back further than the certification — Douglas Shire Council adopted Australia's first local sustainable tourism strategy in 1998, and the area now counts 22+ ECO certified operators, several with 20+ years of continuous Ecotourism Australia certification.
Current pressures
Reef health — the GBR's 2024–25 back-to-back mass bleaching events affect reef trips departing from Port Douglas. Visitor pressure at Mossman Gorge's swimming holes during peak season. Cassowary safety — roadkill and habitat disturbance remain an ongoing concern on rainforest roads.
Before you sell
Check the QPWS conditions report for Daintree National Park before departure — track conditions, closures and stinger-season advice change seasonally. Marine stinger season runs roughly November to May.
04

Biodiversity & environment

Why it matters
This is the only place on the continent where ancient rainforest meets reef directly. The Wet Tropics carries extraordinary endemism built up over hundreds of millions of years of continuous rainforest cover.
Key wildlife
Southern CassowarySaltwater crocodileBennett's tree-kangarooBoyd's forest dragon370+ bird speciesGreen turtleDugong
Species to know
Southern Cassowary
Endangered — a vital seed disperser — over 70 rainforest fruit species rely on it for distribution. Slow down on rainforest roads and never approach or feed one.
Saltwater Crocodile
Protected — present in the Daintree River and estuaries. Never swim, wade or stand at the water's edge in croc habitat.
Bennett's Tree-kangaroo
Endemic — found only in the Wet Tropics rainforest canopy, rarely seen but a genuine local specialty.
Cassowary & crocodile safety
Cassowaries can cause serious injury or death if provoked — never approach or feed one, and slow down through known habitat. The Daintree River holds resident crocodiles — no swimming or wading. At Mossman Gorge, never jump or dive in: currents are unpredictable and people have died there.
05

Getting there sustainably

By air
Cairns Airport (CNS) is the regional gateway, roughly an hour's drive south of Port Douglas along the scenic Captain Cook Highway.
The Daintree River crossing
A cable ferry, not a bridge, carries vehicles across the Daintree River into the national park side. This is a long-standing, deliberate constraint that limits development pressure north of the river — worth explaining to clients as part of the destination's character, not an inconvenience.
On the ground
Small-group, ECO certified tour operators are the preferred choice over large coach tours, given how fragile the rainforest habitat is along key access roads.
06

Accessibility

Overall
Moderate. Port Douglas township and the Mossman Gorge Centre are well set up; deeper rainforest trails and Cape Tribulation beaches are not.
Mossman Gorge
The boardwalk from the Centre to the first viewing platform is wheelchair accessible. The swimming holes and riverside walking track beyond it are not, and carry real currents risk regardless of mobility.
Reef trips
Most Port Douglas reef vessels (including Quicksilver-style operators) offer accessible boarding and onboard facilities — confirm specifics with each operator before booking.
Plan ahead
Confirm accessibility requirements with Mossman Gorge Centre and individual reef and rainforest operators ahead of any booking with mobility needs.
