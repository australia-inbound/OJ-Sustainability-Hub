Staff Position & Scripts

Animal welfare

Where Orange Journeys stands on wildlife experiences — and exactly what to say when a client asks for one we won't book, or books a sensitive one independently.

01

Our position

Orange Journeys does not arrange experiences that compromise animal welfare. As a destination management company, we choose not to package or book wildlife activities that cause animals stress, harm, or disruption to their natural behaviour — even where they remain legal and popular.

This protects the animals, reflects the expectations of a growing share of travellers, and supports Australia's reputation as a responsible wildlife destination. The principles below guide every wildlife booking decision.

01We do not facilitate the holding, handling or restraining of native wildlife for photos or entertainment.
02We prioritise experiences that observe animals in the wild, or in accredited sanctuaries that meet recognised welfare standards.
03We give clients an honest, science-based reason whenever we decline — never a vague refusal.
04We favour operators certified by Ecotourism Australia and those with genuine conservation outcomes.
02

Koala holding

Orange Journeys does not book koala-holding experiences. Holding causes koalas measurable stress, several states prohibit it, and the practice runs counter to our welfare commitment. Use the script below when a client requests it.

Suggested message to the client
"Thank you for asking. As part of our commitment to responsible travel, Orange Journeys doesn't arrange koala-holding experiences — holding causes these animals real stress, and it's actually prohibited in several Australian states. What we'd love to do instead is arrange a visit where you can see koalas up close in a natural setting, often with a keeper talk. It's a more memorable experience, and a kinder one for the koala."
Warm, confident, and offers a better alternative rather than only saying no. Adapt the wording to your own voice — the key is to decline clearly, give the genuine reason, and redirect to a responsible option.
If the client still wants to book it themselves
If a client chooses to arrange a koala-holding experience independently, that is their decision to make. Orange Journeys simply does not package, book or facilitate it on their behalf. Stay warm and non-judgemental — our role is to hold our standard, not to police the client's own choices.
03

Why — the evidence to stand on

If a client asks why, these are the genuine, defensible reasons. They are drawn from welfare science and Australian law — not vague claims a client could challenge.

Koalas are solitary and sleep up to 20 hours a day
They are adapted to minimal interaction, even with other koalas. Being held forces close contact that disrupts their feeding, rest and breeding patterns — all critical to survival.
Handling causes measurable stress
Koalas show signs of distress when removed from their branches and handled in noisy, unnatural settings. A Griffith University study even found koalas accustomed to being held showed stress when visits stopped during COVID lockdowns — evidence the interaction itself shapes their wellbeing.
They are wild animals with sharp claws and teeth
Despite their appearance, koalas can become agitated and scratch or bite when they feel threatened — a risk to the client as well as the animal.
Disease can transmit both ways
Koalas can carry Chlamydia and Koala Retrovirus, and humans can transmit illnesses to koalas — an added welfare risk to an already vulnerable species.
The species is endangered
In February 2022, koala populations in Queensland, NSW and the ACT were uplisted from vulnerable to endangered under the EPBC Act 1999. Queensland's wild koala numbers roughly halved between 2000 and 2020.
04

The legal picture

Where holding stands by state — useful context, though our position applies regardless of local law.

NSW — banned since 1997 Victoria — banned Queensland — permitted, strict rules SA & WA — permitted, strict rules

Where it remains legal, Queensland's rules cap interaction at 30 minutes per day, require mandatory rest days, and prohibit handling females with joeys. The clear national trend is away from the practice — Lone Pine Koala Sanctuary, long the most famous koala-holding venue, ended public holding on 1 July 2024.

A useful reference
One of our own Kangaroo Island suppliers, Exceptional Kangaroo Island, publicly advocates against koala holding and explains why to its trade partners. It's a helpful example of a respected operator taking the same position we do.
05

More experiences clients ask about

Koala holding is the most common request, but it isn't the only one. Below are other popular wildlife experiences, each marked either Won't book — we decline and redirect, as with koala holding — or Book with care, where the experience can be responsible if the client is briefed properly. The same principle applies throughout: we hold our standard and give an honest reason, never a vague no.

Crocodile cage attractions Won't book
The "cage" submersion beside a captive saltwater crocodile uses a wild animal confined for life as a thrill prop. The crocodile cannot express any natural behaviour, and the encounter exists purely for the adrenaline shot.
What to advise instead
Steer clients toward seeing crocodiles in the wild, where the animal is free and the sighting is real — a guided river or wetland cruise delivers close, safe wild crocodile viewing from a boat. Frame it as the better story: a wild croc in its own river, not one in a tank.
Suggested message to decline
"Thanks for asking about that one. As part of our sustainability policy and our commitment to animal welfare, Orange Journeys doesn't book the cage-style croc attractions — it's a wild animal kept in a tank purely for the photo. What I'd recommend instead is a guided river or wetland cruise where you'll see saltwater crocs in the wild, in their own habitat. It's safer, it's a genuine sighting, and honestly it's the better story to bring home."
Captive dolphin swims Won't book
Dolphins in captivity are confined to a tiny fraction of their natural range, and swim-with programs involve handling and performance that welfare science consistently links to chronic stress. Their intelligence and social range make captivity particularly damaging.
What to advise instead
Recommend a wild dolphin experience where the animals are free to come and go — boat-based wild dolphin watching, or a reputable wild-swim operator where dolphins approach the vessel on their own terms and are never fed or touched.
Suggested message to decline
"I'd love to sort a dolphin experience for you, though under our animal welfare commitment we don't book the captive swim-with programs — dolphins range huge distances in the wild, and confinement causes them real stress. The alternative I'd point you to is a wild dolphin trip, where the dolphins choose to come to the boat and aren't fed or handled. You see them behaving naturally, which is the part most people remember."
Quokka selfies Book with care
The quokka is a vulnerable species. On Rottnest the animals are habituated to people, so a respectful photo is genuinely fine — but touching can transmit disease and has been linked to mothers abandoning their young, and human food is harmful to them. Feeding or handling carries on-the-spot fines under the Rottnest Island Regulations, with heavier penalties under Western Australia's Animal Welfare Act.
What to advise if the client books it
Advise the client to crouch down to the quokka's level and let it approach on its own, keep the phone close rather than chasing for the shot, and never touch or feed it. A respectful selfie from ground level gets the photo without the risk — feeding and handling are finable on Rottnest.
Suggested briefing for the client
"Quokkas are lovely and a photo is absolutely fine — just a few things we ask, in line with our animal welfare commitment, to keep them safe and keep you on the right side of the rules. Crouch down to their level and let them wander over to you rather than chasing the shot, keep your phone close, and don't touch or feed them. Feeding actually carries a fine on Rottnest, and human food makes them unwell — but a calm selfie from ground level gets you the photo every time."
Kangaroo & wallaby feeding Book with care
At a reputable free-range sanctuary using its own approved feed, this is a low-impact and genuinely lovely encounter. The harm comes from the wrong context — feeding human or pet food, feeding wild roadside animals (which draws them toward traffic), or crowded venues where animals are overfed.
What to advise if the client books it
Recommend free-range sanctuaries where the venue supplies its own approved feed and the animals roam freely and can walk away. Advise clients never to feed wild kangaroos roadside or bring their own food, and to let the animal choose whether to interact.
Suggested briefing for the client
"Feeding kangaroos can be a really nice encounter as long as it's the right setting. In keeping with our sustainability policy, we'd point you to a free-range sanctuary that provides its own approved feed, where the animals are free to wander off whenever they like. The one thing to avoid is feeding wild roadside kangaroos or bringing your own food — it does them more harm than good. Let them come to you and decide if they're interested."
Turtle nesting & hatchling viewing Book with care
Nesting turtles and hatchlings are highly sensitive to light and disturbance — artificial light disorients hatchlings away from the sea, and flash or close approach can cause a nesting female to abandon her nest and return to the water. Done badly it is harmful; done through a guided program it is one of the best wildlife experiences in Australia.
What to advise if the client books it
Book only guided, ranger-led programs that control group size, lighting and distance. Advise clients: no flash photography, no torches, stay behind the guide, and keep to the rear of the turtle at all times.
Suggested briefing for the client
"Seeing turtles nest or hatch is one of the best things you can do in Australia — and because their welfare matters to us, the key is doing it on a guided, ranger-led tour rather than wandering a beach yourself. They manage the group size, lighting and distance so the turtles aren't disturbed. On the night: no flash, no torches, stay behind your guide, and keep to the back of the turtle. Get that right and it's unforgettable."
