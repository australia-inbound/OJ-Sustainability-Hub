What is coral bleaching?

Coral bleaching occurs when ocean temperatures rise above the threshold that corals can tolerate, typically by just 1–2°C above the seasonal maximum. Under heat stress, corals expel the symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae) living in their tissues — algae that provide corals with up to 90% of their energy through photosynthesis and give them their vivid colour. Without the algae, corals turn ghostly white and begin to starve.

Bleached coral is not automatically dead. If water temperatures return to normal within a few weeks, corals can reabsorb their algae and recover. But if heat stress persists, corals die. In 2024, research tracking 462 coral colonies at One Tree Island found that 66% were bleached by February, 80% by April, and by July 44% of bleached colonies had died. For Acropora corals — among the reef's most iconic species — mortality reached 95% at some sites.

The critical distinction for tour operators: bleaching is not always visible at the surface and conditions vary significantly by location. Always check current GBRMPA reef health reports and work with HSTO-certified operators who conduct regular in-water assessments before directing clients to specific reef sites.
The 2024–2025 bleaching events

The Great Barrier Reef experienced its fifth mass bleaching event in 2024, affecting 74% of all surveyed reefs — the most spatially extensive bleaching event ever recorded on the GBR. Every region of the reef was impacted. This was followed immediately in 2025 by a sixth consecutive mass bleaching event — only the second time in the reef's history it has faced back-to-back bleaching years (the first was 2016–17).

The 2024–25 AIMS Long-Term Monitoring Program — the most comprehensive reef health dataset in the world, spanning 39 years — recorded the largest annual decline in hard coral cover ever measured. 48% of 124 surveyed reefs underwent a decline in coral cover percentage. In some regions, coral cover dropped by up to 30%.

The 2024 bleaching event was part of the fourth global coral bleaching event, which began in February 2023. By March 2025, bleaching-level heat stress had impacted 84% of the world's coral reef areas across 82 countries — the most extensive coral crisis in recorded history.

The climate connection

Ocean temperatures in January–March 2024 were the warmest in 400 years, according to research published in Nature using coral core palaeoclimate records. Human-caused climate change is directly responsible for the warming that drives bleaching events — climate model analysis confirms this unambiguously.

Mass bleaching events have occurred six times on the GBR since 2016, compared to just twice in the previous two decades. The interval between events is shrinking — the reef no longer has time to fully recover before the next bleaching season arrives. Scientists describe this as "increased volatility," a sign of an ecosystem under sustained stress.

AIMS researchers have stated clearly: "The future of the world's coral reefs relies on strong greenhouse gas emissions reduction, management of local and regional pressures, and development of approaches to help reefs adapt to and recover from the impacts of climate change and other pressures."

The reef is currently under active UNESCO World Heritage monitoring. In 2023, the Australian Government committed AUD $4.4 billion in reef protection investments specifically to address UNESCO concerns about the reef's long-term viability.
Impact on tourism

The Great Barrier Reef contributes approximately AUD $6.4 billion to the Australian economy annually and supports around 64,000 jobs. It is one of Australia's most powerful drawcards for international visitors.

Mass bleaching events create a significant challenge for reef tourism. Heavily bleached areas offer a degraded visual and ecological experience for snorkellers and divers — the vivid colours, abundant fish life and healthy coral formations that visitors come to see are diminished or absent. Operators working in heavily impacted areas report reduced client satisfaction and increased pressure on alternative, healthier reef sites.

Paradoxically, some responsible tourism operators have found that well-briefed clients who understand the bleaching context come away with a deeper appreciation of the reef's significance and a stronger commitment to conservation. The Tourism Reef Protection Initiative — a program involving tourism operators in active reef health monitoring — conducted 25,671 reef health surveys in 2023–24, demonstrating that the industry can be part of the solution.

There are also signs of resilience. Approximately 16% of tracked coral colonies showed recovery after the 2024 bleaching peak, demonstrating that where conditions stabilise, coral can bounce back. Not all of the reef bleached equally — some sections remain in good health, and the overall picture is one of a reef under severe stress but not lost.

Recovery programs & positive action

Significant investment is going into reef restoration and adaptation. The Reef Restoration and Adaptation Program (RRAP) is developing large-scale coral restoration techniques including coral spawning interventions — deploying millions of young corals back onto damaged reef areas. The Indigenous Futures project partners Traditional Owner Sea Rangers with AIMS scientists to lead restoration on their own sea Country.

The Crown-of-Thorns Starfish Control Program — which compounds bleaching damage — culled 11,640 hectares of reef habitat in 2023–24. The Reef 2050 Long-Term Sustainability Plan and the Blueprint for Climate Resilience 2030 provide the strategic frameworks guiding Australia's reef management response.

What this means for Orange Journeys
  • Only contract GBRMPA High Standard Tourism Operators (HSTOs) — they conduct regular reef health surveys and operate responsibly in sensitive areas
  • Check GBRMPA seasonal reef condition reports before each group departure and avoid directing clients to actively bleaching or recovering sites
  • Brief clients on the bleaching context honestly — informed clients have better experiences and become advocates for reef conservation
  • Encourage participation in the Great Reef Census (September–December) — clients can contribute to citizen science while visiting
  • Direct per-booking contributions to the Great Barrier Reef Foundation or Reef Restoration Foundation
  • Ensure all clients receive the reef visitor code of conduct — sunscreen, touching, wildlife distances — before water activities
  • Preference Master Reef Guide-accredited tours for sensitive reef sites — 82 guides are currently accredited across the reef
