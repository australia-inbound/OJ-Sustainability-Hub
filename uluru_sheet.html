Destination overview

Location & size
Uluṟu is a sandstone monolith 348m high and 9.4 km around, in the southern Northern Territory. With the domes of Kata Tjuṯa 40 km west, it forms Uluṟu–Kata Tjuṯa National Park — a UNESCO dual World Heritage Site for both natural and cultural value.
Character
One of the most sacred landscapes on Earth for its Aṉangu Traditional Owners. A place of profound cultural and spiritual significance, not simply a natural landmark — this framing must shape every client interaction.
Access
Fly to Ayers Rock Airport (AYQ) at Yulara — direct services from Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Cairns and Alice Springs. There is no practical alternative to flying for most visitors — an honest limitation of the destination.
Best season
May–September (cooler months) — best for walking, daytime temperatures manageable. December–February — extreme heat, walks close above 36°C. Sunrise and sunset are the iconic viewing times year-round.

02

Aṉangu Traditional Owners & Tjukurpa

Traditional Owners
The Aṉangu are the Traditional Owners of Uluṟu–Kata Tjuṯa, with a continuous culture stretching back tens of thousands of years. The park is jointly managed by Aṉangu and Parks Australia, with traditional law guiding decisions.
Tjukurpa
Tjukurpa is the foundation of Aṉangu culture — law, knowledge, religion, and the relationships between people, land and all living things. It governs every aspect of how the land is cared for and how visitors should behave.
Climbing is permanently prohibited
Climbing Uluṟu has been banned since 26 October 2019. The climb crossed a sacred men's ceremonial track tied to Tjukurpa. This is a legal prohibition, not a preference — communicate it to every client without exception.
Priority recommendation
Begin every itinerary at the Cultural Centre, and include an Aṉangu-guided experience such as the Maruku Arts dot-painting workshop or a SEIT Outback Australia cultural tour led by Traditional Owners.
03

Sustainability & current status

Cultural sustainability
The greatest sustainability priority at Uluṟu is cultural, not environmental. Respecting Tjukurpa, photography restrictions and sacred-site closures is the core responsibility of every visitor and operator.
Photography rules
Some areas of Uluṟu are permanently off-limits to photography — marked by signs — because they depict sacred sites. Always check signage, and always ask permission before photographing Aṉangu people. Commercial photography requires a Parks Australia permit.
Environmental care
Stay on marked tracksCarry out all rubbishDon't remove rock or soilRespect water sourcesFeral animal & buffel grass management
Before you sell
Brief clients on cultural protocols before arrival — not on the day. Set expectations that climbing is banned, that some sites cannot be photographed, and that this is a place of living culture deserving deep respect.

Environment & landscape

Geology
Uluṟu is the visible tip of a vast rock slab extending kilometres underground. Its colour shifts dramatically with the light — most spectacularly at sunrise and sunset — due to iron oxidation on its surface.

Kata Tjuṯa
The 36 domes of Kata Tjuṯa rise higher than Uluṟu. The Valley of the Winds and Walpa Gorge walks pass through them — terrain here is rugged and walks close in extreme heat.
Heat & safety — critical
Uluṟu's extreme heat (up to 50°C) is a genuine medical risk. Walks close automatically above 36°C. The nearest hospital is Alice Springs — 450 km away. Ensure clients carry water (1L per hour), sun protection, and travel insurance with medical evacuation cover.
05

Getting there sustainably

Primary access
Fly to Ayers Rock Airport (AYQ) at Yulara — there is no practical low-carbon alternative for most visitors. Prefer direct flights over connections via Alice Springs where possible.
The Ghan
Some travellers combine Uluṟu with Alice Springs and join The Ghan (Adelaide–Darwin) for part of a longer itinerary — a meaningful lower-carbon option for the overland leg.
On the ground
A free shuttle connects Yulara resort, airport and key sites. Hiring a car is common for reaching multiple sunrise/sunset viewing areas. No public transport runs between Yulara and the park sites.
Honest framing
Uluṟu is the most transport-challenged destination in the portfolio due to its remoteness. Acknowledge this with clients and recommend carbon offsetting for the flight legs as standard practice for this itinerary.
06

Accessibility

Overall
Good for a remote desert destination — most key experiences are accessible with planning.
Accessible walks
The Kuniya Walk (1km return to Mutitjulu Waterhole) and Mala Walk (2km return) are on flat, maintained paths suitable for wheelchairs, with deep cultural significance. The full Uluṟu Base Walk (10.6km) is accessible on maintained sandy paths — a powered wheelchair or carer is recommended for the full circuit.
Cultural Centre
Fully accessible with ramps, paved paths and accessible facilities. Recommended as the first stop for every visitor.
Viewing areas
All sunrise and sunset viewing areas are wheelchair accessible with paved surfaces and seating. The Valley of the Winds and Walpa Gorge walks at Kata Tjuṯa are not accessible — terrain is rugged.
Heat & medical risk
Extreme heat presents real risk for visitors with cardiovascular or heat-sensitive conditions. The nearest hospital is 450 km away. Clients with relevant conditions should consult a physician before travel and carry evacuation cover.
