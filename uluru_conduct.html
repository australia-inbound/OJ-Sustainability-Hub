Visitor Guide · Travelling Responsibly

Uluṟu–Kata Tjuṯa

We can't wait to welcome you to Uluṟu–Kata Tjuṯa. A few simple things will help you visit safely and respectfully, in a place of deep living culture.

Four things to know before you go
01
Climbing Uluṟu is permanently banned
Banned since October 2019, the climb crossed a sacred men's ceremonial track tied to Tjukurpa. This is a legal prohibition, not a preference, and applies to every client without exception.
02
Respect photography restrictions
Some areas of Uluṟu cannot be photographed because they depict sacred sites, always follow signage. Never photograph Aṉangu people without explicit permission.
03
Start at the Cultural Centre
Understanding Tjukurpa first transforms the whole visit. Encourage every client to begin at the Cultural Centre and to choose an Aṉangu-guided experience.
04
Prepare for extreme heat
Heat is a genuine medical risk, up to 50°C. Walks close above 36°C. The nearest hospital is 450km away. You'll need 1L of water per hour, sun protection, and evacuation cover.
Please take extra care
Heat and remoteness. Please note that walks close automatically above 36°C, the nearest hospital is in Alice Springs (450km away), and travel insurance with medical evacuation cover is essential, particularly for those with heat-sensitive conditions.
Visiting Uluṟu–Kata Tjuṯa responsibly
Encourage
  • Use the name Uluṟu, not Ayers Rock
  • Stay on all marked tracks
  • Check and respect no-photography signs
  • Ask permission before photographing Aṉangu
  • Carry 1L of water per hour in heat
  • Visit the Cultural Centre first
  • Buy art from Aṉangu-owned outlets only
  • Observe all park and Sorry Business closures
Advise against
  • Climb Uluṟu or any rock formation
  • Photograph or enter restricted sacred sites
  • Remove rock, sand, soil or plant material
  • Enter the Mutitjulu community
  • Walk alone in remote areas
  • Attempt walks above 36°C
  • Litter anywhere in the park
  • Buy souvenirs from non-Aṉangu outlets
Aṉangu cultural respect
Uluṟu–Kata Tjuṯa is a place of living culture, jointly managed by its Aṉangu Traditional Owners. We'd encourage you to engage with Tjukurpa through Aṉangu-guided experiences such as Maruku Arts workshops and SEIT cultural tours.
For park status and closures: parks.australia.gov.au/uluru. Verify before every departure. Reviewed at least every 12 months.
