Visitor Guide · Travelling Responsibly

Tasmania

We can't wait to welcome you to Tasmania. A few simple things will help you explore it safely, and help protect what makes it so special.

Four things to know before you go
01
Drive carefully at dawn, dusk and night, everywhere
Wildlife collisions are a leading cause of death for the endangered Tasmanian devil. Treat every rural road, especially around Cradle Mountain, Freycinet and the Tasman Peninsula, as active habitat.
02
Respect biosecurity at every entry point
Tasmania's strict rules on fresh produce, plants and soil protect an island ecosystem still free of several mainland pests and diseases.
03
Stay on marked tracks in the wilderness
Much of the Tasmanian Wilderness World Heritage Area is remote and fragile, holding both natural and 40,000-year-old Aboriginal cultural values.
04
Acknowledge palawa/pakana Country
The island's First Peoples have an unbroken 40,000-year connection here, through the last ice age. Use lutruwita place names alongside English where known.
Please take extra care
Wildlife & weather. Never drive at dawn, dusk or night if avoidable, as collisions are a major cause of Tasmanian devil deaths. Check Tasmania Fire Service alerts before any remote or wilderness departure, year-round.
Exploring Tasmania responsibly
Encourage
  • Acknowledge palawa/pakana Country
  • Use lutruwita and other place names alongside English
  • Choose Aboriginal-led experiences where available
  • Drive cautiously at dawn, dusk and night
  • Declare all fresh produce and plants at biosecurity checkpoints
  • Stay on the Dove Lake boardwalk and other marked tracks
  • Pack layers for sudden weather changes
  • Check Tasmania Fire Service alerts before remote departures
  • Support small-group and certified wilderness operators
Advise against
  • Speeding on rural roads at dawn, dusk or night
  • Bringing undeclared fresh produce or plants
  • Leaving marked tracks in wilderness areas
  • Feeding any wildlife, including devils and quolls
  • Trapping or relocating devils or quolls, as it's illegal
  • Lighting fires outside designated areas in summer
  • Climbing beyond barriers at Wineglass Bay or coastal lookouts
  • Disturbing historical or cultural sites
  • Ignoring track closure or fire-danger signage
palawa/pakana Country
lutruwita carries an unbroken 40,000-year Aboriginal story, including significant Pleistocene-era cultural sites. We'd encourage you to choose Aboriginal-led experiences such as the wukalina Walk or palawa kipli food experiences.
For fire alerts: fire.tas.gov.au. For park conditions: parks.tas.gov.au. Reviewed at least every 12 months.
